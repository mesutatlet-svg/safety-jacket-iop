Safety Jacket


1.  What's a Safety Jacket?
    A safety jacket, also known as a hi-vis jacket or vest, is gear that helps people be seen easier. It can also protect you from weather and stuff.

2.  Why Use Them?
    *   So people can see workers when it's dark, the weather's bad, or it's just a risky area.
    *   To stop accidents with cars, machines, etc.
    *   To follow the rules at work.
    *   Some keep you warm, block chemicals, stop fire, or resist cuts.

3.  What to Look For
    *   Bright colors like yellow, orange, or lime green.
    *   Reflective tape that shines when light hits it (like from headlights).
    *   Made of stuff like polyester, cotton, PVC, mesh, or warm stuff for winter.
    *   Closes with a zipper, Velcro, or snaps.
    *   Pockets for tools and pens.
    *   Some block rain and wind, or keep you warm.

4.  Types of Safety Jackets
    *   Hi-Vis Vests/Jackets: Light and good for general visibility.
    *   Traffic Safety Jackets: Super reflective for working near roads.
    *   Construction Jackets: Tough and built to last.
    *   Industrial Jackets: Can block chemicals or fire.
    *   Thermal/Cold Weather Jackets: Warm for working outside in the winter.
    *   Rainproof Jackets: Keep you dry with sealed seams.
    *   Multi-Standard Jackets: Hi-vis plus fire-resistant or anti-static stuff.

5.  Rules for Safety Jackets
    Different countries have their own rules.
    *   ANSI/ISEA 107 (USA): Jackets are rated Class 1, 2, or 3 depending on how much they cover and how reflective they are.
    *   EN ISO 20471 (Europe): Rules for hi-vis clothing.
    *   OSHA Guidelines (USA): Says you need hi-vis gear for some jobs.

    Protection Classes (ANSI):

    *   Class 1: Not too risky (parking guys, warehouse workers).
    *   Class 2: Medium risk (road work, utility workers).
    *   Class 3: Very risky, fast traffic, or low light (highway workers, emergency people).

6.  Where to Wear Them
    *   Road work
    *   Construction sites
    *   Airports and seaports
    *   Factories and warehouses
    *   Emergency crews (police, firemen, paramedics)
    *   Mines and oil/gas fields
    *   Train and subway work

7.  How to Keep Them Up
    *   Wash them like the tag says (too much washing makes them less reflective).
    *   Check them often for rips, fading, or worn strips.
    *   Get a new one if it's not reflective enough or the fabric is torn.

8.  Good Things About Them
    *   Keeps workers safer
    *   Some are light and comfy
    *   Helps companies follow the law
    *   Not too expensive

9.  What They Can't Do
    *   Won't stop you from getting hit or stabbed
    *   Get less reflective when dirty, worn, or washed
    *   Not enough for dangerous stuff like chemicals or fire (unless they're made for that)
